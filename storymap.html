The gap

What existing metrics can't see

Caltrans tracks delay, throughput, and crashes. All the right things for what they measure. But none of them capture the everyday lived experience of a neighborhood next to a corridor running 20 mph over its posted limit. A road that's free-flowing can be free-flowing precisely because it's going too fast — and that shows up nowhere in our current reporting.

The SMP Prosperity goal P.SA.01.20 asks us to reduce excessive motor vehicle speeds on SHS surface facilities in developed areas by 2028 — using NPMRDS or similar data. Item a, due 2025, is to define and identify those instances. That's what this is.
What we currently track
  • → Peak-hour delay and LOS
  • → Crash outcomes and severity
  • → Vehicle throughput
  • → VMT and mobility performance
What this work adds
  • → Persistent upper-tail speed exposure
  • → Everyday operating conditions — not just crash events
  • → Where system behavior ≠ community context
  • → Time-of-day and residential-area specificity
Safe Systems lens: Harm occurs when speeds exceed what surrounding human contexts can safely absorb. That's a QoL burden independent of whether a crash has happened yet.
Methodology

From averaged summaries to actual observed behavior

The core shift: instead of pre-processed aggregated speed metrics that smooth out the upper tail, we go directly to individual NPMRDS time-stamped observations — filtering for readings at the 90th and 95th percentile and above, which is exactly what aggregated averages erase.

Speed tier classification
TierThresholdDefinition
Normal≤75 mphAND speed/reference < 1.3×. Within expected range.
High75–85 mphAND speed/reference ≥ 1.3×. Elevated — concerning on surface streets.
Extreme≥85 mphAbsolute threshold. Dangerous regardless of context.
Anomaly≥ 2× postedMost alarming — e.g. 70 mph on a 35 mph residential street.
Pipeline
1NPMRDS passenger vehicle data, April–September (6 months). Do not use single-month — it underrepresents persistent extremes.
2Classify each reading into speed tiers. Count readings per tier per TMC segment.
3Buffer SHS 50ft → spatial join → intersect → length-weighted conflation to SHS segments.
4Join context: NLCD land use (500m buffer → urban_pct), CDE schools, ACS population, EQI.
5Compute exposure score → tier → hotspot map + ranked corridor table.
Exposure score formula
severity_signal =
  (pct_anomaly × 2.0)
  + (pct_extreme × 1.5)
  + (pct_high × 1.0)
exposure_score =
  severity_signal
  × lu_residen
  × log(population / 1000)
  × 100 → normalized 0–100
Anomaly weighted 2× because speed ≥ 2× posted limit on a surface street represents the most severe behavioral mismatch — independent of absolute speed. Residential land use and population answer "who is exposed."

The institutional argument

What traditional metrics miss

Free-flow is not the same as appropriate. This corridor operates without congestion precisely because it operates too fast for its residential surroundings. That condition is invisible in LOS — and it shows up clearly here. This is the case for adding speed exposure as a QoL performance metric alongside the existing suite.
What this replaces

Nothing. This is additive. The delay and crash data remain the primary metrics for their respective purposes. Speed exposure is a complementary layer that surfaces conditions those metrics can't see.

What it enables

A ranked list of SHS segments where operating conditions are inconsistent with surrounding context — which is exactly the evidence base needed to write the 2026 abatement plan and prioritize the 2028 project plans.
